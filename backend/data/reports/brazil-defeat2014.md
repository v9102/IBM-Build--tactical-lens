# 2014 World Cup Semi-Final — Brazil 1-7 Germany

## Formations and tactical shape
Brazil's 4-2-3-1 was missing both its defensive lynchpin (Thiago Silva, suspended) and its creative talisman (Neymar, injured). Dante replaced Silva but had zero chemistry with David Luiz, whose aggressive pressing left gaping holes. Germany's 4-2-3-1 exploited this ruthlessly — Müller roamed centrally from the right, Kroos and Khedira overran Fernandinho and Paulinho in midfield, and Lahm's underlapping runs created endless overloads.

## Why the early goals mattered
Germany scored in the 11th minute through Müller from a corner — a set-piece goal that forced Brazil to chase the game. With their emotional captain Silva missing and their crowd hero Neymar absent, Brazil had no reference point. The second goal (23', Klose) was a tap-in after a save, breaking the World Cup all-time scoring record and visibly deflating the team. What followed was a psychological collapse: four goals in six minutes.

## The decisive collapse
Brazil's high press, usually coordinated by Silva's leadership, became chaotic. Full-backs Marcelo and Maicon pushed high without cover, and David Luiz's wild forward runs left Dante exposed. Germany bypassed the press with one-touch combinations and found runners in behind repeatedly. Kroos' two goals in 69 seconds (24', 26') were scored from central areas where Fernandinho had vacated his position — a catastrophic midfield discipline failure.

## Momentum read
The match turned on two factors: the early set-piece goal and Brazil's emotional frailty. The iconic image of David Luiz in tears before half-time summed up the psychological collapse. Germany's efficiency reached its peak in a six-minute spell that was the most dominant attacking display in World Cup semi-final history. The momentum never shifted back — Brazil were broken in the first half.
