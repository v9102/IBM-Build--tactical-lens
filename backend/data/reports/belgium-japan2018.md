# 2018 World Cup Round of 16 — Belgium 3-2 Japan

## Formations and tactical shape
Belgium's 3-4-2-1 had dominated possession all tournament, but Japan's compact 4-2-3-1 caught them cold. Japan pressed high in the first half, forcing errors from Belgium's back three — the weakest point of their system. Belgium's wing-backs (Meunier, Carrasco) were caught upfield repeatedly, and Japan exploited the wide spaces with quick switches to Inui and Haraguchi.

## Why the 2-0 deficit was structural
Belgium's risk profile was inherently high: Vertonghen and Alderweireld were slow in transition, and the midfield duo of Witsel and De Bruyne offered limited defensive cover. Japan's first goal (48') came from a quick transition after a wasted Belgium corner — exactly the vulnerability scouted pre-match. Japan's second (52', Inui) was a individual moment of quality from range, but the space Inui received should have been closed by Belgium's holding midfielders.

## The turning point — Martinez's subs
Roberto Martínez introduced Fellaini and Chadli together at 65', switching to a 4-3-3 with Fellaini as a target man. The tactical shift was immediate: Fellaini's presence drew both Japanese centre-backs, creating space for Vertonghen's looping header (69') and then equalising himself from a cross (74'). Japan's defensive block, so disciplined for 65 minutes, could not handle the aerial and physical mismatch.

## Momentum read — the perfect counter
Japan pushed for a winner in stoppage time and won a corner. When Courtois caught it, Belgium transitioned with a 4-on-4 that Martinez had drilled repeatedly. De Bruyne's 60-metre sprint, Hazard's composure, Lukaku's intelligent decoy run (standing still to free Chadli), and Chadli's finish — the sequence was not luck but design. It took 14 seconds from catch to goal, the fastest counter-attack in World Cup knockout history.
